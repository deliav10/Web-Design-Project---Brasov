The History of Brașov

Through the Ages

1211: Foundation

In 1211, by order of King Andrew II of Hungary, the Teutonic Knights fortified the Burzenland to defend the border of the Kingdom of Hungary. On the site of the village of Brașov, the Teutonic Knights built Kronstadt – the City of the Crown. Although the crusaders were evicted by 1225, the colonists they brought in long ago remained, along with local population in three distinct settlements they founded on the site of Brașov: Corona, around the Black Church (Biserica Neagră); Martinsberg, west of Cetățuia Hill; Bartholomä, on the eastern side of Sprenghi Hill.

14th Century: Trade Hub

In the 14th century, Brașov grew significantly as an important trade hub, benefiting from its strategic location along trade routes between Central Europe and the Ottoman Empire. Saxon settlers, invited by the Hungarian kings, built the first fortifications around the city, which helped defend it against frequent invasions. Brașov became an integral part of the Saxon settlement in Transylvania, known for its bustling markets and growing economy.

1689: The Great Fire

In 1689, Brașov was struck by a devastating fire that caused massive destruction in the heart of the city. The blaze consumed large parts of the town, including significant buildings such as the St. Mary's Church. The fire's aftermath left the church severely damaged, and the smoke-blackened walls gave it the name "Black Church," a title it still holds today. This tragic event led to the reconstruction of the city with sturdier materials like stone and reinforced urban planning. The fire also had a profound impact on the city's architecture, marking a turning point in Brașov’s development as it shifted from wood-based structures to more durable ones.

19th Century: Cultural Flourishing

The 19th century marked a period of significant transformation for Brașov, as it experienced rapid industrial and cultural growth. During this time, Brașov was part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, which greatly influenced its development. The city became an important industrial hub, particularly for textiles and machinery.

At the same time, Brașov saw a flourishing of its cultural scene, with the opening of theaters, schools, and cultural institutions. The city's diverse population, which included Saxon, Hungarian, and Romanian communities, contributed to a rich cultural mix.

20th Century: Modern Era

The 20th century marked a period of significant transformation for Brașov. After World War I and the Union of Romania in 1918, the city underwent modernization and industrialization, which boosted its economy. During the interwar years, Brașov saw growth in cultural and architectural developments.

In the post-World War II era, under Communist rule, the city experienced a period of rapid industrialization, though this was often accompanied by the demolition of older buildings to make room for new factories and infrastructure. The city's historical charm mixed with industrial progress, defining its identity during this era.

During the Romanian Revolution of 1989, Brașov was one of the key cities that saw significant unrest. Workers protested against the oppressive Communist regime, leading to clashes with security forces. The city's involvement played a crucial role in the downfall of the regime, contributing to Romania’s transition to democracy.
